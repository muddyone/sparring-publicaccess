## Recommendation

**Adopt a scope-bounded hybrid anchored on Path B (language-first) with a structured 2-week concepts-first front-load and explicit pseudocode/diagram scaffolds carried through the Python weeks.** Concretely: weeks 1-2 introduce algorithmic decomposition, control-flow semantics, and list/dict mental models in pseudocode plus state diagrams (Path A's intrinsic-load-only entry); weeks 3-16 follow Path B's syntax-and-build cadence, but every new construct is introduced first as a pseudocode/diagram fragment and then translated to Python in the same session. Programming projects begin week 5-6 (preserving the adult-learner "I built something" milestone), and the final 4 weeks include an explicit "concept-not-language" capstone unit (e.g., re-expressing a Python solution in pseudocode and arguing about complexity) to serve the 38% who continue. This is a Path-B-shaped course with Path-A scaffolding rather than a 50/50 split, because NCCC's specific demographic and historical data tilt the balance toward Path B and the cost of getting completion wrong is higher than the cost of getting the transferability delta wrong.

## Reasoning chain

The decision must be made under genuine evidence-base tension, and the hybrid resolves it by direction rather than by averaging.

**Why the front anchor is Path B, not Path A.** Three of the six evidence items point at Path B for *this population*: the andragogy literature on adult learners (item 3) explicitly names early concrete competence as a motivational anchor, and 57% of NCCC's CS101 cohort is adult learners — this is not a small subgroup but the modal student. The Hatfield et al. finding (item 4) suggests that earlier syntactic fluency may close the open-ended-coding gap for first-gen and ESL students (together ~31% and ~20% of the cohort, with non-trivial overlap); even discounting for the Lewis et al. 2022 non-replication, the directional implication still favors getting these students into syntax sooner rather than later. The NCCC-specific historical data (item 6) shows that the 2018 Python switch — a syntactic-overhead reduction within an existing Path B structure — produced a 7-9 point completion-rate jump and a stable 73% rate, which is the closest local analog NCCC has. None of this is dispositive, but the local-evidence vector points one way.

**Why the front anchor is not pure Path B.** Cognitive-load theory (item 1) is theoretically clean about what happens when you stack new conceptual models and new symbolic notation simultaneously: measurable degradation of concept formation. Path B in its pure form does exactly that in week 1. The transfer-program data (item 5) finds a small but real Path-A advantage (d ≈ 0.18-0.22) for the subset who take data structures within 18 months of transfer — a non-trivial fraction of the 38% continuing students. A pure Path B leaves both of these on the table when a 2-week pseudocode/diagram front-load can capture much of the cognitive-load benefit at relatively low schedule cost.

**Why the hybrid is asymmetric (Path B-heavy) rather than 50/50.** The Bennedsen & Caspersen meta-analysis (item 2) is the most important single piece of evidence for *not* over-investing in the architectural choice: across 63 CS1 courses, sequencing did not predict completion-rate differences; preparation and language choice did. This argues against the high-variance pure-Path-A bet and for a structure that gets the Path-B motivational and accessibility wins while taking only a 2-week excursion to capture cognitive-load theory's structural insight. The expected value of the hybrid is dominated by what we know works locally (Python + early competence) plus a structural prophylactic against known failure modes (concept-and-syntax simultaneity in week 1).

**Counterfactual concerns the pack does not name but that the methodology section flags as fair game.** Two unstated factors push further toward Path B-anchored: first, NCCC has 4 FT faculty and 6-8 adjuncts per semester (item context), and adjuncts in community-college CS often have engineering rather than pedagogy backgrounds; Path A's pseudocode-and-diagrams pedagogy is harder to execute consistently across 14 sections taught by 10-12 instructors than Path B's "type this, run it" pedagogy. The instructor-training cost and consistency risk of pure Path A is real and is not captured in the published literature. Second, the 62% who do not continue need the course to deliver transferable general-education value — computational thinking — which is genuinely better served by working code experience plus structured concept reflection than by 5 weeks of pseudocode that they will not see again. The hybrid's week-13-16 capstone is specifically designed to deliver the concept-discipline outcome to the non-continuing cohort.

**On the central tension between the 62% and the 38%.** The hybrid does not resolve this; it accepts that the 62% completion-and-general-ed priority outweighs the 38% transferability priority by raw count and by reversibility (a low completion rate is harder to recover from than a 3-4 percentage-point downstream data-structures delta). The capstone unit is the offering to the 38%; it is not a full Path A substitute.

## Concerns / risks

- **The hybrid does not solve the transfer-program delta.** Smith & Garcia 2021 and Park et al. 2023 found a small but consistent Path-A advantage in early-transfer data structures performance. The 2-week pseudocode front-load and the week 13-16 capstone will likely recover *some* of that effect but probably not all of it. NCCC should be explicit that the 38% continuing population is being asked to accept a small expected disadvantage in exchange for a much larger expected gain for the 62% non-continuing population.
- **Item 4's replication status is not settled.** I leaned on Hatfield et al. 2019's directional implication; Lewis et al. 2022 failed to replicate. If Lewis is right, the first-gen/ESL syntactic-fluency benefit of Path B may be smaller than I claimed. The recommendation does not collapse without item 4 — items 3 and 6 still point the same way — but the strength of the case is partly load-bearing on a contested finding.
- **Hybrid execution risk.** Hybrids are easy to design and hard to teach. With 6-8 adjuncts per semester, the "every construct introduced first as pseudocode then translated" discipline will erode without explicit syllabus templates, instructor training, and probably a shared lecture-deck repository. If NCCC adopts the hybrid in spec but cannot resource the consistency, it will degrade to "Path B with extra weeks of confusion in week 1-2" — strictly worse than pure Path B.
- **No evidence specifically about *this* hybrid.** Items 2 and 5 are about pure-architecture comparisons. There is a plausible reading where 2-week pseudocode front-loads are dominated by either pure architecture and have been quietly tried and abandoned in the literature; the pack does not surface counter-evidence and I did not pull it independently.
- **Andragogy as a category may be too coarse.** "Adult learners" at NCCC includes returning workforce learners, displaced workers, and second-career students; their motivational profiles are not identical, and Knowles 1980 is treated as more settled in the pack than in current adult-education literature.

## Confidence

- **Path B should be the structural anchor for NCCC specifically: High.** Three independent evidence items (3, 4, 6) plus the NCCC-specific completion-rate sensitivity all point this way; item 2 explicitly says architectural choice does not dominate, which lowers the cost of being wrong about the architectural detail and raises the relative weight of the demographic fit.
- **Cognitive-load theory justifies a pseudocode front-load: High on the theory, Moderate on the magnitude in practice.** Sweller et al. 1998 is well-established and the simultaneity-of-load problem is real, but extrapolating to "2 weeks captures most of the benefit" is my judgment, not a finding from the pack.
- **The transfer-program advantage is small and partially recoverable: Moderate.** Cohen's d ≈ 0.18-0.22 is real but small; the early-vs-late distinction in item 5 narrows the affected population further. Whether the capstone unit recovers it is unverified.
- **Hybrid will execute as designed across 14 sections: Low.** This is the load-bearing operational risk and the place where the recommendation is most likely to fail in practice. NCCC's staffing model is a real constraint and the recommendation does not budget for it.
- **The 62%-vs-38% trade-off resolution favors completion: Moderate-to-High.** The asymmetric stakes (a low completion rate triggers departmental concern at <75%; a small downstream delta does not trigger anything visible) and the 1.6:1 population ratio both argue for prioritizing completion, but this is a values call as much as an evidence call and a legitimate alternative reading exists.
