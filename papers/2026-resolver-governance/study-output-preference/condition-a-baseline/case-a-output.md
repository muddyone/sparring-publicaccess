## Recommendation

**Adopt Option 2 ("team members") as the canonical handbook term, paired with a one-paragraph "Definitions and Legal Classification" clause near the front of the handbook that names "employee" as the legal-classification term used in offer letters, payroll, tax filings, and any document referencing the at-will employment relationship.** This is a scope-bounded hybrid: "team members" carries the rhetorical and cultural register throughout the ~28,000 words of substantive handbook copy, while "employee" is preserved verbatim in the legal/classification clause and any quoted statutory or contractual passage. Reject Option 3 ("colleagues") for the handbook's primary term despite the committee chair's case; the cost-benefit on the available evidence does not support it.

## Reasoning chain

The strongest single signal is the convergence of three independent data streams on "team members." TerraClarity has used "team members" externally for 7 years (evidence 4), 67% of staff prefer it in a near-census internal survey (evidence 6), and 22% of comparable mid-sized professional-services firms use it as their primary handbook term (evidence 3) — making it the second-most-common industry choice and well within convention. No other option has all three streams pointing the same direction. "Employees" wins industry convention (71%, evidence 3) but loses external-voice consistency and gets only 12% staff preference. "Colleagues" wins on the committee chair's compensation-philosophy argument (evidence 5) but is a 4% industry outlier (evidence 3), gets 21% staff preference (evidence 6), and would introduce a *third* internal/external term mismatch on top of the existing two (evidence 4).

The Pfeffer finding (evidence 1) cuts in favor of "team members" over "employees" on perceived-hierarchy grounds, but with effect sizes of 1-3 percentage points. This is real but modest evidence; it should tip a close call, not override stronger signals. Crucially, Pfeffer's specific replicated comparison was "team members" vs. "employees" — there is no cited finding that "colleagues" outperforms "team members" on the same outcome. The committee chair's argument for "colleagues" is therefore extrapolation beyond the cited evidence, not grounding in it.

The legal-document consistency issue (evidence 2) is load-bearing and must be addressed regardless of choice. The cited *Toussaint*-line doctrine is clear: handbook term choice does not by itself alter at-will classification, but inconsistency between handbook and offer letter can be cited as ambiguity in wrongful-termination disputes. Evidence 2 itself supplies the resolution: "handbook can use any term as long as a clarifying clause links to the formal employment-classification term used in legal documents." A single definitions clause — for example: "Throughout this handbook, 'team members' refers to the people who work at TerraClarity. For purposes of employment classification, tax reporting, payroll, and the at-will employment relationship governed by your offer letter, the legal term is 'employee'" — discharges this risk at trivial cost. This clause must exist whichever option is chosen; it is not specific to my recommendation, but it is mandatory under any of them.

A counterfactual concern the pack does not name: the committee chair championing "colleagues" is also the chair of the partnership compensation committee — i.e., the body whose decisions most visibly produce non-peer outcomes (pay-band placement, performance-based progression). Adopting that chair's preferred peer-register term in the handbook, against a 67% staff preference for a different term, risks reading as register-management by the very person whose authority the term is meant to flatten. This is a reputational/credibility concern distinct from the lexical choice itself, and it sharpens the case against Option 3 even before the evidence is weighed.

A second counterfactual concern: transition cost. "Team members" requires zero changes to the website, client proposal templates, or kickoff decks (evidence 4), and the handbook draft change is a global find-replace plus a definitions clause. Either of the other options carries non-trivial cascading update cost across already-printed and externally-published materials, or accepts an ongoing inconsistency. This is a small but real factor the pack does not surface.

On the survey itself (evidence 6): the non-anonymous design is a methodological weakness — staff may have under-reported preference for "employees" (the term that connotes hierarchy) when answering identifiably to leadership. This *strengthens* rather than weakens the case for "team members," since the 67% plurality emerged despite a design that, if anything, would inflate the socially-desirable peer-register answers. The 21% "colleagues" share is the most likely number to be inflated by social desirability and proximity to the chair's known position.

## Concerns / risks

1. **Pfeffer effect sizes are small.** A 1-3 percentage point shift in survey-measured perceived hierarchy is unlikely to be felt as a strategic outcome. The recommendation's cultural payoff is therefore modest; do not oversell it to the partnership.

2. **The recommendation does not solve the underlying disagreement on the partnership compensation committee.** The chair's view that "colleagues" register aligns with open-pay-band philosophy is a substantive position. Adopting "team members" overrides that position on weight-of-evidence grounds but does not address it. The chair may reasonably want a separate venue to make the philosophy argument (e.g., a values statement section that uses "colleagues" once, in context, with explanation) — but this risks the mixed-approach outcome that only 3% of firms adopt and that creates internal inconsistency.

3. **Non-anonymous survey design.** Evidence 6's preference distribution may be biased. The 67% number is directional but not precise. If staff preference were truly close to 50/50, the case for Option 2 would weaken — but the recommendation would not flip, because external-voice consistency and industry convention still favor "team members."

4. **The definitions clause is necessary-but-not-sufficient legal hygiene.** Evidence 2 names the consistency issue but does not name other handbook-related employment-law risks (e.g., implied-contract claims from policy language, jurisdictional variation in at-will doctrine). A real handbook adoption should have employment counsel review beyond the lexical-consistency question. The recommendation does not substitute for that review.

5. **Transition for new hires already onboarded under "Operating Norms."** The 9-page Google Doc precursor uses some mix of terms. A small communication moment ("we've standardized on 'team members' in the new handbook, with 'employee' reserved for legal-classification contexts") avoids surprising existing staff. The recommendation assumes this happens; it is not free.

6. **No mention in the pack of unionization or collective-bargaining context.** In some jurisdictions and industries, "employee" is a term of art under labor law (e.g., NLRA "employee" classification). For a 50-person environmental-consulting firm in 2026, this is unlikely to be live, but it is not zero. Employment counsel review should confirm.

## Confidence

- **High**: That Option 3 ("colleagues") should be rejected as the handbook's primary term. Three independent streams (industry convention 4%, staff preference 21%, third internal/external mismatch) converge against it; the only strong argument for it is the committee chair's philosophy claim, which is not grounded in cited evidence and carries a credibility risk given the chair's role.

- **High**: That a definitions/legal-classification clause is required regardless of lexical choice. Evidence 2 states this directly and supplies the resolution mechanism.

- **Moderate**: That Option 2 ("team members") beats Option 1 ("employees") for the primary term. The 67% staff preference, 7-year external-voice consistency, and Pfeffer's directional finding all point this way; the counterweight is industry convention (71% use "employees") and the legal-document default. The hybrid resolves the legal concern, leaving the choice to weigh culture/voice (favors Option 2) against following-the-pack (favors Option 1). I land on Option 2 but acknowledge a defensible Option 1 recommendation exists for a more conservative firm.

- **Moderate**: That the non-anonymous survey design biases the result toward "team members" / "colleagues" over "employees" rather than between the peer-register options. This is a judgment call about social-desirability dynamics, not a measured effect.

- **Low**: That the committee chair's advocacy for Option 3 should be characterized as a conflict-of-interest concern rather than a substantive philosophy disagreement. I flagged it as a counterfactual concern worth surfacing, but a charitable reading is simply that the chair is the partner who has thought hardest about compensation register and is advocating in good faith. The pack does not give enough information to discriminate between these readings.
