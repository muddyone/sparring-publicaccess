## Recommendation

**Adopt a generative-first single-mode architecture for NCCC's CS101 redesign — closer to Path B than to Path A, but rejecting Path A's 5-week defer-coding cliff and Path B's bare language-first sequencing both.** Concretely: project-based work as the spine throughout the 16-week semester; weeks 1-3 are the inclusion+attitude+validation window with Guzdial Media Computation-style contextualized tasks (image manipulation, CSV from a domain students care about, simple text processing); every student ships a working artifact they wrote themselves by end of week 2, with week 1 containing at least a smaller student-authored running artifact ("hello, your name, three lines you wrote") to prevent the validation event from slipping. Concept reinforcement layers *inside* the project work as construct-specific 20-30 minute trace-and-predict micro-passes — when a project introduces a new construct (loops, nested data, functions-as-parameters, recursion), the warmup runs as load management before the project work that uses it, NOT as a separate weekly mode. Single mode means no weekly handoff between pseudocode-mode and code-mode; one shared bank of construct-warmups; adjuncts pull the warmup that matches their section's current construct. Design the course for the 62% terminal cohort as primary (computational literacy = read+modify existing code, real tooling, domain-relevant projects); serve the 38% transfer cohort with a second-course bridge (CS1.5 or honors track inside CS2). Name the 73%/75% completion-rate concern explicitly as a pipeline problem the architecture cannot solve, and pair the curriculum recommendation with three concrete structural interventions: corequisite math support replacing prerequisite developmental math, embedded-tutor advising touchpoints in weeks 3 and 6, and proactive intervention triggers tied to first-assignment non-submission.

## Reasoning chain

The decision sits inside two distinct evidence bases — CS-education research and andragogy / community-college pedagogy — and the recommendation works only when both are honored. Sweller, van Merriënboer & Paas (1998), *Educational Psychology Review* 10(3), 251-296, on cognitive-load theory provides the theoretical case for separating intrinsic load from extraneous load early, but van Merriënboer & Kirschner (2018) *Ten Steps to Complex Learning*'s 4C/ID model explicitly recommends whole-task sequencing with progressive scaffolding, NOT a five-week pure-concept prelude. Path A's defer-coding cliff overgeneralizes from "separate the loads" to "separate them for five weeks," which the cognitive-load literature itself does not endorse and which the andragogy literature actively contradicts.

Andragogy is decisive on the early-validation timing. Wlodkowski & Ginsberg (2017) *Enhancing Adult Motivation to Learn* (4th ed.) place experienced competence inside the inclusion+attitude window — first 2-3 weeks. Tinto (2012) *Completing College: Rethinking Institutional Action* locates highest community-college attrition risk in the first 4-6 weeks. Bailey, Jaggars & Jenkins (2015) *Redesigning America's Community Colleges* cite the "first three weeks" as the dominant predictor of one-semester persistence at community colleges. Rendón (1994) "Validating Culturally Diverse Students," *Innovative Higher Education* 19(1), 33-51, and Rendón (2002), *Educational Policy* 16(4), 642-667, on validation theory require validation to be a *generative act the student did themselves* — not a receptive exercise. For NCCC's 57% adult-learner / 31% first-gen / 20% ESL demographic, a week-4 generative milestone falls past Tinto's integration-critical window and Wlodkowski/Ginsberg's experienced-competence requirement; week 2 is the latest defensible target, with week 1 contributing a smaller artifact to keep the inclusion window open.

Pure Path B has its own gap. Soloway & Spohrer (1989) *Studying the Novice Programmer* and Pea (1986), *Journal of Educational Computing Research* 2(1), 25-36, on language-independent conceptual bugs document that novices form plan-composition misconceptions that pure language-first pedagogy tends to leave intact. Pea's "superbug" — the assumption that the program "knows" what the programmer intends — is conceptual, not syntactic, and Python's low syntactic overhead (case evidence 6) does not address it. The construct-specific trace-and-predict micro-passes resolve this: Lopez et al. (2008) *ICER 2008* and Lister et al. (2004) ITiCSE Working Group Reports (the BRACElet hierarchy) show that targeted tracing on specific misconceptions delivers a measurable transfer effect. The Smith & Garcia (2021) and Park et al. (2023) finding of small-but-real Path-A advantage in early-transfer data structures performance (Cohen's d ≈ 0.18-0.22) attaches to construct-specific reinforcement, not to architecture-as-mode — so the warmup architecture preserves it without the defer-coding cliff.

Bennedsen & Caspersen (2007), "Failure rates in introductory programming," *ACM SIGCSE Bulletin* 39(2), 32-36, is the most important constraint in the case and is decisive on architecture-vs-completion: their 63-course meta-analysis finds completion-rate variance dominated by student preparation and language choice, NOT sequencing. This means the architectural choice is largely *not load-bearing for completion*, which is NCCC's stated concern (the 73%/75% threshold). The honest inference: architecture choice will not move completion past 75% by more than a few points; what moves completion is structural intervention. Bailey, Jaggars & Jenkins (2015) Ch. 6-7 are concrete on which structural levers actually move community-college aggregate completion: corequisite remediation replacing prerequisite developmental math, proactive advising touchpoints, default course-sequence pathways with milestone checks. Achieving the Dream seven-state intervention data and Calcagno et al. (2008) *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis* 30(2), 155-170, on first-generation outcomes corroborate: course-pedagogy interventions show small, often non-significant aggregate-completion effects; structural interventions show medium-to-large effects. The recommendation accordingly names 73%/75% as a pipeline problem and pairs the curriculum architecture with three concrete structural interventions; the curriculum choice does not claim to move aggregate completion past 75% on its own.

Designing for the 62% terminal cohort as primary follows Bailey et al.'s majority-population principle: when the terminal cohort exceeds 50%, terminal-cohort design dominates. NCCC is 62%/38%. Guzdial Media Computation's "computing for everyone" framing (Guzdial 2003 onward, with replications across community-college contexts) supports this — contextualized generative work in week 1 produces both higher pass rates AND higher CS-major continuation among under-represented populations. Margolis & Fisher (2002) *Unlocking the Clubhouse* on belonging in CS, Cheryan, Plaut, Davies & Steele (2009) *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 97(6), 1045-1060, on ambient classroom cues, and Steele & Aronson (1995) *JPSP* 69(5), 797-811, on stereotype threat all converge: a course that signals "you are a legitimate generative participant from week one" outperforms a course that signals "you are doing pre-programming before programming" — particularly for first-gen and ESL students reading classroom cues for membership signals.

The transfer cohort (38%) gets the deeper conceptual primitives via a second-course bridge — CS1.5 or an honors track inside CS2 — carrying recursion, AST-level reasoning, Felleisen *How to Design Programs* design recipes. The bridge depends on articulation agreements with receiving four-year institutions (Crisp & Nora 2010, *Community College Review* 37(4), 291-322): articulation friction is where community-college transfer cohorts are typically lost, so articulation work is a prerequisite for the architecture, not a follow-on.

The single-mode adjunct-coordination architecture follows from Eagan & Jaeger (2009) *Research in Higher Education* 50, 168-188, and Jaeger & Eagan (2011) *Research in Higher Education* 52, 511-538: students with greater part-time-faculty exposure show reduced persistence and transfer, with mechanisms including curricular drift across sections. Bailey et al. (2015) Ch. 5 documents that complex hybrid pedagogies degrade across adjunct pools when redesigns require coordinated weekly handoffs between modes — and CCCSE benchmark data shows cross-section variance in adjunct-heavy gateway courses is the single largest correlate of cross-section completion variance. Single mode means one project-based pedagogy with construct-warmups pulled in as needed; the adjunct decision is "is my section on loops this week? pull the loop warmup," not "what mode should I be in this week?" The bank requires named owner, misconception-tag metadata on each warmup tied to the Soloway/Spohrer/Pea localized misconception literature, and a default selection rule when adjuncts have not diagnosed misconceptions in their section.

## Concerns / risks

1. **Bank curation quality.** "Pull the loop warmup" only delivers the Lopez/BRACElet transfer effect if the warmup is genuinely misconception-targeted, not a generic loop exercise. The bank requires a named owner, misconception-tag metadata per warmup, and a default selection rule when the adjunct hasn't diagnosed misconceptions. Without these, the construct-specificity that delivers the transfer effect collapses. Cohen & Brawer's *American Community College* literature consistently flags this kind of curation labor as undercapacity at most community colleges; treat it as an explicit institutional commitment, not an assumption.

2. **Validation-event contextualization quality.** Architecture cannot mandate that adjuncts pick domains students actually care about. A "ship a working artifact in week 2" milestone delivered without contextualization (e.g., "print Hello World, then print your name, then print 1+1") meets the architectural target but fails the Rendón validation requirement. Adjunct selection of context matters; instructor selection of context is the load-bearing operational variable the architecture cannot directly control.

3. **Articulation friction for the transfer-cohort second-course bridge.** CS1.5 / honors-CS2 only works if (a) it actually exists in the catalog, (b) advising routes transfer-bound students into it, and (c) receiving four-year institutions accept it as articulation-equivalent. Articulation work is a prerequisite, not a follow-on; the recommendation should not be adopted without an explicit commitment to articulation effort.

4. **Adjunct framing fidelity for load-management warmups.** The construct-warmup framing only holds if adjuncts deliver it as load management within real work the student already owns. If introduced as "let's understand loops before we use them," it collapses back to PAS-flavored sequencing — the very pattern the architecture rejects. Instructor script and adjunct training language must explicitly name this framing distinction.

5. **The recommendation does not close the pipeline gap.** Bailey et al. (2015), Margolis et al. (2008) *Stuck in the Shallow End*, and ATD data are unambiguous that course-level redesign reliably fails to move aggregate completion at open-admission community colleges. The three paired structural interventions (corequisite math, embedded tutoring, intervention triggers) are the curriculum-adjacent levers within recommendation scope, but the broader pipeline problem (financial aid friction, work-schedule conflicts, placement-test misassignment, dependent-care, prerequisite math preparation) sits outside curriculum scope entirely.

6. **The transfer-cohort delta is not fully recovered.** Smith & Garcia (2021) and Park et al. (2023) found a small-but-consistent Path-A advantage in early-transfer data structures performance. The construct-warmup architecture and the second-course bridge will likely recover *some* of that effect but probably not all of it. NCCC should be explicit that the 38% continuing cohort is being asked to accept a small expected disadvantage in exchange for a larger expected gain for the 62% non-continuing cohort.

## Confidence

- **High**: Generative-first in weeks 1-3 is the right architecture for NCCC's specific demographic. Three independent evidence streams (Wlodkowski/Ginsberg's experienced-competence requirement; Tinto's integration-critical window; Rendón's validation-must-be-generative) plus the Margolis/Fisher and Cheryan belonging literature all converge.

- **High**: 73%/75% is a pipeline problem, not a curriculum problem. Bailey et al., Margolis et al., and ATD intervention data all align; Bennedsen & Caspersen's null sequencing finding from inside the CS-ed literature corroborates from a different evidence base.

- **High**: Single-mode architecture reduces adjunct-coordination risk versus a multi-mode hybrid. Eagan & Jaeger, Bailey Ch. 5, and CCCSE benchmark data all point this way; the structural argument is straightforward.

- **High**: Designing for the 62% terminal cohort as primary follows Bailey et al.'s majority-population principle and serves the under-represented populations the Margolis/Fisher and Cheryan belonging literature identifies as most sensitive to early-classroom signals.

- **Moderate**: Construct-specific trace-and-predict micro-passes preserve the Lopez/BRACElet transfer effect. The BRACElet effect was measured in tracing-as-mode contexts; embedding it as load-management warmups is a reasonable but not directly tested derivative.

- **Moderate**: Second-course bridge serves the transfer cohort adequately. Depends on whether CS1.5/honors-CS2 actually exists, is advised early, and articulates cleanly to receiving four-year institutions. The articulation prerequisite is real and is a partner commitment, not an architectural property.

- **Moderate**: The three paired structural interventions are the right ones. Bailey Ch. 6-7 names them as high-leverage; Achieving the Dream data corroborates; but interventions of this kind have institution-specific implementation friction NCCC will need to absorb.

- **Low**: Quantification of how much of the transfer-cohort delta the construct-warmup architecture and second-course bridge will recover. The path is plausible but the magnitude is unverified.
