## Recommendation

Use "team members" as the single primary term throughout the handbook — but anchor it legally in two layers, not one. Define it on first use ("team members — referred to as 'employees' in TerraClarity's legal and payroll documents"), and in the at-will and termination sections carry the explicit pairing "employees (team members)" so the legally operative word is unambiguous exactly where a wrongful-termination dispute would look. Reject "colleagues."

## Reasoning chain

The decisive case is fit, not popularity. TerraClarity has used "team members" in every outward-facing surface for seven years, and new hires meet that term before they ever see "employees" (evidence 4). Choosing it removes a standing internal/external mismatch instead of adding one; "colleagues" would create a third mismatch across legal documents, outward voice, and handbook.

The 67% staff preference (evidence 6) corroborates this — but it should be read carefully, not oversold. The survey was answered under staff's own names, not anonymously, and the "team members" write-ins ("everyone says it now," "fits how the company already talks") are voice-conformity tells, not independent endorsement. So 67% is plausibly a ceiling, and it confirms the voice-continuity case (evidence 4) rather than independently carrying the decision.

"Colleagues" is rejected on converging grounds: only 4% of 217 peer firms use it (evidence 3), only 21% of staff preferred it (evidence 6), and Pfeffer (evidence 1) finds word choice moves *perceived* hierarchy by just 1–3 points while changing no actual authority or reporting lines — so "colleagues" cannot deliver the flattened hierarchy its advocate wants. That perceptual effect is kept small and directional, not inflated.

## Concerns / risks

- The two-layer legal remedy carries a real cost: the belt-and-suspenders "employees (team members)" pairing makes the at-will and termination sections read more bureaucratically than the warm "team members" voice elsewhere — a visible tonal seam the handbook will carry.
- It overrides the compensation chair's stated preference (evidence 5) — a political cost inside the partnership that a vocabulary choice will not smooth.
- It departs from the 71%-of-peer-firms "employees" convention (evidence 3); an outside lawyer skimming the handbook may read "team members" as softer or less precise than expected.
- The perceived-hierarchy gain is small (evidence 1). Structure and the published pay-band practice — not the word — must do the cultural work.

## Confidence

Medium-high. The choice rests on seven years of voice consistency (evidence 4), corroborated (not carried) by the staff survey (evidence 6), and the one genuine legal exposure — the handbook-vs-offer-letter mismatch evidence 2 warns about — is neutralized by anchoring the operative term redundantly where a dispute would point. The residual uncertainty is political (the chair) and tonal, not evidentiary.
