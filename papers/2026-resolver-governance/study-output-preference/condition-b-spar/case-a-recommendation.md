## Recommendation

**Adopt Option 2 ("team members") as TerraClarity's canonical handbook address term, with a narrow hybrid: use "employee" as the operative term in legally-load-bearing sections, plus a residual catch-all clause covering "any section implementing a federal or state statutory regime that uses 'employee' as its operative term."** Include a clarifying front-matter clause that explicitly integrates terminology equivalence with the at-will employment relationship — drafted as: *"Throughout this handbook, TerraClarity uses 'team member' as its customary cultural usage. For all purposes of employment classification, payroll, tax withholding, benefits eligibility, statutory rights and obligations, and any reference to applicable federal, state, or local employment law, 'team member' and 'employee' have identical meaning and refer to the same legal status as defined in your offer letter and applicable law. Nothing in this handbook's use of 'team member' is intended to create, and shall not be construed as creating, any contract of employment, any guarantee of continued employment, or any modification of the at-will employment relationship described in [Section X]."* Include Section 7 savings clauses as required architecture in rule sections that interact with NLRA-protected concerted activity. Reject Option 3 ("colleagues") for the handbook's primary term despite the committee chair's case; cross-disciplinary evidence (organizational-behavior + employment-law) points consistently against it.

## Reasoning chain

The case for "team members" rests on convergence across two distinct evidence bases: organizational-behavior research and U.S. employment-law doctrine. From the management-science side, three independent signals favor "team members" — TerraClarity's 7-year external-voice consistency (evidence 4), 67% staff preference in the near-census internal survey (evidence 6, with the non-anonymous-survey caveat that this discounts but does not eliminate the signal), and 22% industry-convention support (evidence 3, second-most-common). The Pfeffer 2007 finding (evidence 1) provides directional support with effect sizes of 1-3 percentage points on perceived hierarchy, and notably its replicated comparison was specifically "team members" vs. "employees" — there is no cited finding that "colleagues" outperforms "team members" on the same outcome. Adopting "colleagues" would generalize beyond the available evidence and introduce a third internal/external term mismatch on top of the two TerraClarity already manages.

A structural concern about Option 3 sharpens the case against it: the partnership compensation committee chair, whose body's decisions most visibly produce non-peer outcomes (pay-band placement, performance-based progression), is the chief advocate for the peer-register term. Adopting that term against a 67% staff preference for a different term risks reading as register-management by the very authority the term is meant to flatten. This is reputational/credibility risk distinct from the lexical choice. The legal dimension corroborates: under *Clackamas Gastroenterology v. Wells*, 538 U.S. 440 (2003), partner-vs-employee status under ERISA / FICA / state wage law turns on common-law agency factors, and *Vizcaino v. Microsoft*, 120 F.3d 1006 (9th Cir. 1997) (en banc) shows how labeling can collide with reality and produce retroactive participant status. "Colleagues" framing is admissible totality evidence in misclassification disputes; vocabulary alone does not determine status, but it is not zero, and the Generator's intuition about a structural problem is corroborated by the legal landscape.

The legal-document consistency issue (evidence 2) is load-bearing under the *Toussaint*-line implied-contract doctrine: *Toussaint v. Blue Cross & Blue Shield of Michigan*, 408 Mich. 579 (1980); *Pine River State Bank v. Mettille*, 333 N.W.2d 622 (Minn. 1983); *Woolley v. Hoffmann-La Roche, Inc.*, 491 A.2d 1257 (N.J. 1985); *Asmus v. Pacific Bell*, 23 Cal.4th 1 (2000); *Foley v. Interactive Data Corp.*, 47 Cal.3d 654 (1988); *Guz v. Bechtel National*, 24 Cal.4th 317 (2000); *Weiner v. McGraw-Hill*, 57 N.Y.2d 458 (1982). Handbook term choice does not by itself alter at-will classification, but inconsistency between handbook and offer letter can be cited as ambiguity in wrongful-termination disputes — and *Toussaint* specifically treats "failure to negate a manual's apparent commitments" as actionable. *Woolley* requires disclaimers be "prominent and unmistakable." *Nicosia v. Wakefern Food Corp.*, 136 N.J. 401 (1994), and *Witkowski v. Thomas J. Lipton*, 136 N.J. 385 (1994), further require typographic conspicuousness (bold/capitalized/bordered typography), separately-signed acknowledgment, and verbatim disclaimer repetition at cross-reference sites — placement-and-repetition without typographic conspicuousness has been litigated against employers and lost. The clarifying clause as drafted above addresses both terminology equivalence AND foreclosure of the implied-promise reading; mere terminology equivalence is insufficient.

The hybrid boundary requires explicit enumeration. Federally-prescribed "employee" terminology applies to: FMLA general notice (29 C.F.R. § 825.300(a)(3)); ADA reasonable-accommodation procedures (29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(f)); anti-harassment policy structure (per *Faragher v. City of Boca Raton*, 524 U.S. 775 (1998), *Burlington Industries v. Ellerth*, 524 U.S. 742 (1998), and EEOC's April 29, 2024 Workplace Harassment Enforcement Guidance); exempt-classification disclosures (29 C.F.R. § 541.2; DOL Field Operations Handbook ch. 22; *Encino Motorcars v. Navarro*, 138 S. Ct. 1134 (2018)); independent-contractor-facing sections (29 C.F.R. § 795 under the 2024 final rule, 89 Fed. Reg. 1638); I-9/IRCA (8 U.S.C. § 1324a); USERRA (38 U.S.C. §§ 4301-4335; 20 C.F.R. § 1002.7); ERISA § 3(6) (29 U.S.C. § 1002(6)) and SPDs (29 C.F.R. § 2520.102-3); OSHA recordkeeping (29 C.F.R. Part 1904); IRC payroll/withholding (26 U.S.C. § 3402, FICA); SOX (18 U.S.C. § 1514A) and Dodd-Frank (15 U.S.C. § 78u-6) whistleblower channels; non-FMLA statutory leaves including PWFA (42 U.S.C. § 2000gg with EEOC April 2024 final regs), jury duty, and military leave; and harassment-investigation confidentiality. The residual catch-all clause captures these without exhaustive enumeration and accommodates surfaces not anticipated above; it is recommended over exhaustive enumeration as the more durable boundary.

Section 7 savings clauses are required architecture under *Stericycle, Inc.*, 372 NLRB No. 113 (2023), and NLRB GC Memo 23-08 (Oct. 31, 2023) and GC 24-01, which establish that handbook rules with a "reasonable tendency to chill" Section 7 protected concerted activity are presumptively unlawful. "Team member" framing in collective-identity-flavored rules amplifies this risk because the rhetorical mechanism that delivers engagement-uplift in cultural sections is the same mechanism the Board has flagged as chilling when it appears in confidentiality, civility, social-media, solicitation, recording/photography (*Whole Foods Market*, 363 NLRB 800 (2015), enf'd 691 F. App'x 49 (2d Cir. 2017)), off-duty access (*Tri-County Medical Center*, 222 NLRB 1089 (1976)), investigation-confidentiality during pendency, dress code and insignia (*Home Depot USA*, 373 NLRB No. 25 (2024)), electronic communications (*Purple Communications*, 361 NLRB 1050 (2014)), or severance/arbitration carve-outs (*McLaren Macomb*, 372 NLRB No. 58 (2023)). Each rule section must include explicit Section 7 reservations including the right to discuss wages, hours, and working conditions.

State-law overlay matters but does not change the recommendation's vocabulary direction. California's *Foley* totality-of-circumstances inquiry (Cal. Lab. Code §§ 200 et seq., 432.3, 432.6, 1102.5, 2750 et seq.), Montana's WDEA (Mont. Code Ann. §§ 39-2-901 to 39-2-915) requiring discharge consistency with handbook provisions, and N.Y. Lab. Law § 201-d / § 201-g all increase the cost of sloppy execution but do not force a different lexical recommendation. Drafting precision in Montana matters more than in any other jurisdiction because the WDEA reads handbook provisions expressly.

## Concerns / risks

1. **Tonal inconsistency from the hybrid is a real cost the clarifying clause mitigates but does not eliminate.** Switching between "team member" and "employee" within the same document creates a register break; internal-brand research treats voice consistency as a cultural asset. The trade-off is buying engagement uplift at the price of register breaks at rights-bearing sections, which is the correct trade in a rights-heavy regulatory environment but worth being explicit about.

2. **Pfeffer effect sizes are small (1-3 pp).** A 1-3 percentage-point shift in survey-measured perceived hierarchy is unlikely to be felt as a strategic outcome. The recommendation's cultural payoff is therefore modest and should not be oversold to the partnership.

3. **The recommendation does not solve the underlying compensation-committee-chair disagreement.** The chair's view that "colleagues" register aligns with open-pay-band philosophy is a substantive position. Adopting "team members" overrides that position on weight-of-evidence grounds but does not address it. A separate values-statement section that explains the philosophy without using "colleagues" as the canonical address term is the cleaner accommodation.

4. **Drafting precision matters more than vocabulary in some jurisdictions.** Montana's WDEA reads handbook provisions expressly; in that jurisdiction every substantive policy provision matters more than the lexical wrapper around it. The recommendation does not change this but raises the stakes on execution quality.

5. **The clarifying clause is necessary-but-not-sufficient legal hygiene.** This recommendation does not substitute for full employment-counsel review of the handbook (implied-contract claims from policy language across all rule sections, jurisdictional variation in at-will doctrine, ERISA participant-status risk, full NLRA implications). The recommendation's scope is the lexical choice and the load-bearing legal architecture; the broader handbook review is its own scope.

6. **Three implementation choices remain partner-decision-shaped after this recommendation:**
   - Whether to enumerate the hybrid-boundary sections exhaustively or rely on the residual catch-all clause (the catch-all is recommended).
   - Whether to adopt typographic-conspicuousness specifications for the front-matter clause (strongly recommended in *Toussaint*-line jurisdictions).
   - Whether the Section 7 savings architecture covers all post-*Stericycle* rule categories the Board has flagged or just the highest-frequency five (recommended: cover all; the Section 7 environment makes omissions costly).

## Confidence

- **High**: Option 3 ("colleagues") should be rejected as the handbook's primary term. Three management-science signals (industry convention 4%, staff preference 21%, third internal/external mismatch) plus the legal *Clackamas*-adjacent ERISA misclassification-evidence concern converge against it; the only strong argument for it is the committee chair's philosophy claim, which is not grounded in cited evidence and carries credibility risk given the chair's role.

- **High**: A clarifying front-matter clause with explicit at-will integration is required regardless of vocabulary choice. *Woolley*'s "prominent and unmistakable" standard plus *Toussaint*'s "negation of apparent commitments" rule together require both terminology equivalence AND explicit foreclosure of implied-promise reading. Mere terminology equivalence is insufficient.

- **High**: Section 7 savings clauses are required architecture, not optional polish, in rule sections interacting with NLRA-protected concerted activity. *Stericycle* and GC 23-08/24-01 make this explicit.

- **Moderate**: Option 2 ("team members") beats Option 1 ("employees") for the primary term. The 67% staff preference, 7-year external-voice consistency, and Pfeffer's directional finding all point this way; the counterweight is industry convention (71% use "employees") and the legal-document default. The hybrid resolves the legal concern, leaving the choice to weigh culture/voice (favors Option 2) against following-the-pack (favors Option 1). The recommendation lands on Option 2 but acknowledges a defensible Option 1 exists for a more conservative firm.

- **Moderate**: The non-anonymous survey design biases the result toward "team members" / "colleagues" over "employees" rather than between the peer-register options. This is a judgment call about social-desirability dynamics, not a measured effect.

- **Moderate**: The residual catch-all clause is the cleanest enumeration approach. It covers known federally-prescribed-vocabulary surfaces without requiring exhaustive listing and accommodates surfaces not anticipated. The trade-off is reduced concreteness for HR drafters, who must judge case-by-case whether a given section "implements a federal or state statutory regime that uses 'employee' as its operative term." Exhaustive enumeration is more concrete but harder to maintain as new statutes and regulations are added.
