# Case A — Organizational vocabulary

## question
TerraClarity Consulting is a 50-person environmental-consulting firm with a flat structure (four founding partners, ~46 staff). It's finishing its first employee handbook (~28,000 words), going to all staff on July 1, 2026; the decision is due June 15, 2026. The question: throughout the handbook, what single word should it use for the people who work there? Three options: "Employees" (the word in the firm's legal documents — offer letters, payroll — and the industry standard); "Team members" (what the firm has used in its outward-facing materials for 7 years); or "Colleagues" (proposed by the partner who chairs the pay committee, to signal a flat, peer relationship). The draft currently uses "Employees," but the pay-committee chair says that clashes with the firm's outward voice and its pay philosophy. Recommend a word (or a justified hybrid), with reasoning grounded in the evidence, the trade-offs it does not solve, and how to handle the legal-document consistency issue.

## evidence_summary
Six pieces of evidence: (1) Word choice at work (Pfeffer 2007). The words in formal company documents measurably shift how staff and outsiders perceive the firm, but the effect is small (1–3 points) and changed no actual authority or reporting lines. (2) Legal consistency (the Toussaint v. Blue Cross line). The word itself doesn't change at-will status; the risk is a mismatch — if the handbook and offer letters use different words, the other side in a wrongful-termination dispute can cite the inconsistency. A one-line clause linking the handbook term to the legal term removes the risk for any choice. (3) What peer firms do (SHRM 2024, 217 firms): 71% "employees," 22% "team members," 4% "colleagues," 3% deliberately mixed — i.e., "employees" is the common norm, "team members" a sizable minority, and "colleagues" rare. (4) The firm's own voice: "team members" for 7 years in outward materials, and new hires meet it before "employees" — so the handbook word either matches the established outward voice or introduces an internal/external mismatch. (5) Pay philosophy: the firm uses published pay ranges with performance-based progression, and the pay committee's 2025 notes tie the "colleagues" framing to that approach. (6) Staff preference: a September 2025 survey — answered non-anonymously, under staff members' own names (43 of 46 staff) — found 12% preferred "employees," 67% "team members," and 21% "colleagues."

# Case B — Introductory programming curriculum

## question
Northern Coastal Community College (a public two-year college, ~6,400 students a year, ~1,100 in computer science) is redesigning its CS101: Introduction to Programming for 2026-27, with a decision due June 30, 2026. Its students: ~57% adult learners (age 22+), ~31% first-generation, ~20% second-language; about 73% finish — already just under the 75% line the department watches, so how many students finish is a live part of this decision, not a side detail. Python is the teaching language. Two ways to build the course: Path A "concepts first" — weeks 1–5 teach the thinking (breaking problems down, organizing data, controlling the order of steps) in plain sketches and diagrams, with no code yet; Python starts week 6. Path B "language first" — weeks 1–5 teach Python itself with small runnable programs; the deeper thinking emerges from coding later. Which path better serves the course's goals (especially "understand programming as a discipline, not just one language")? Recommend a path (or a justified hybrid), with reasoning, the trade-offs it does not solve, attention to that student body, and the split between the 38% who continue (transfer prep) and the 62% who don't (finishing + general value). Staffing: 4 full-time faculty + 6–8 adjuncts across ~8 sections.

## evidence_summary
Six pieces of evidence: (1) Mental load (Sweller 1998). Teaching a new way of thinking and a new notation at the same time measurably hurts learning versus one at a time — the theory-based case that concepts-first lowers the load while students form the ideas. (2) What drives finishing (Bennedsen & Caspersen 2007, 63 courses). Completion varied across a 65–78% band by course design and a wider 58–85% band by language — and the study found finishing depended far more on students' preparation and on the language than on concepts-vs-language ordering; ordering by itself did not predict completion. (3) How adults learn (Knowles 1980). Adults are motivated by early, concrete competence, which favors language-first's "a working program by week 3"; concepts-first's code-free weeks 1–5 give adult learners nothing to run until week 6 — which point (1) argues lowers their mental load while the ideas form, but may cost motivation earlier. (4) First-gen & second-language students (Hatfield 2019). They trail about 12 points on write-from-scratch tasks; earlier syntax practice may narrow that gap — but the finding failed to replicate in Lewis 2022. (5) After transfer (Smith & Garcia 2021; Park et al. 2023). A concepts-first edge appeared only for students who took data structures within 18 months of transferring, and vanished for everyone else; where it held it was a small effect — d ≈ 0.18–0.22, about 3–4 points on the final. (6) NCCC's own history. The 2018 Java→Python switch — a language change, not a path change — raised completion from 64% to 73%. That gain came from changing the language, not the ordering; there is no data on what a Path A switch would do.
