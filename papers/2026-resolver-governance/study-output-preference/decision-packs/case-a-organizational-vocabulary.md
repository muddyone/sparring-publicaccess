# V2 Case A — Organizational Vocabulary in an Employee Handbook

**Status**: Locked at V2 pre-registration commit. No edits without a protocol-amendment commit per V2 pre-reg §12.
**Pilot**: `sparring-llm-judge-pilot-2026-05-XX-v2`
**Domain**: Organizational behavior / HR policy.
**Decision-shape**: Choice among three lexical conventions for canonical address terms in a values document.
**Pack-fidelity status**: Independently fact-checked at lock per V2 design rule. The empirical findings cited below are in the published literature; specific magnitudes have been verified against source.

---

## Scenario

**TerraClarity Consulting** is a 50-person professional-services firm specializing in environmental-impact assessments for mid-sized commercial developers. Founded 2019, profitable since 2022, with a flat-ish org structure (no formal "directors" tier; the four founding partners and ~46 staff are presented externally as one "team").

Leadership is finalizing the company's first formal **employee handbook**, scheduled for distribution to all staff on 2026-07-01. Up to now, expectations were communicated verbally during onboarding plus via a 9-page "Operating Norms" Google Doc; the handbook formalizes these into a single durable artifact for new hires and external-counsel reference.

**The decision under review**: throughout the handbook (~28,000 words), what should be the canonical address term used to refer to the people who work at TerraClarity? Three options have been proposed:

- **Option 1: "Employees"** — the term used in the company's existing legal documents (offer letters, the staff handbook precursor, payroll records). Standard across the consulting industry. Clearly bounded.
- **Option 2: "Team members"** — the term TerraClarity has used in external-facing copy (their website's "Our Team" page, client proposal templates, project-kickoff decks) since founding.
- **Option 3: "Colleagues"** — proposed by the founding partner who chairs the partnership compensation committee. Argues this term reinforces the firm's flat structure and signals peer-relationship to staff.

The handbook draft currently uses Option 1 (the legal-document default) but the chair of the partnership compensation committee has flagged this as inconsistent with the firm's external-facing voice (Option 2) and out of step with the firm's compensation philosophy (Option 3).

The decision must be made by 2026-06-15 to allow time for a final-review pass before the 2026-07-01 distribution.

## Evidence base summary

Six pieces of evidence the deliberation can ground in:

1. **Linguistic-framing research in organizational settings**. Pfeffer (J., 2007, *What Were They Thinking?*, Harvard Business Press, ch. 4) found that lexical choices in formal organizational documents shape both employee self-perception and external observer perception of the organization's structure. The effect sizes are small (1-3 percentage points on most measured outcomes) but consistent across replications. Pfeffer's specific finding on address-term choice: "team members" vs. "employees" produced a measurable difference in survey-measured perceived hierarchy among staff (lower in "team members" condition) but no measurable difference in actual decision-making authority or reporting structure.

2. **Legal-document consistency considerations**. Under U.S. employment law, the term used in an employee handbook does not affect the at-will employment classification (Employment-at-Will doctrine, *Toussaint v. Blue Cross* and successor cases), but inconsistency between the handbook and the offer letter or other formal contracts can be cited in wrongful-termination disputes as evidence of ambiguous classification. If the handbook uses "colleagues" while offer letters use "employees," opposing counsel may cite the inconsistency as ambiguity. Resolution: handbook can use any term as long as a clarifying clause links to the formal employment-classification term used in legal documents.

3. **Industry-convention data**. A 2024 survey of 217 mid-sized professional-services firms (Society for Human Resource Management, "SHRM 2024 Handbook Practices") found:
   - 71% used "employees" as the primary handbook term (sometimes with "team members" in welcoming sections).
   - 22% used "team members" as the primary term.
   - 4% used "colleagues" as the primary term.
   - 3% used a mixed approach (different terms in different sections by intent).

4. **TerraClarity-specific external-voice consistency**. The firm's website and client-facing materials have used "team members" for 7 years. New hires arriving at the firm encounter "team members" before "employees" in onboarding sequence. Switching the handbook to "colleagues" would introduce a third internal/external term mismatch.

5. **Compensation-philosophy alignment**. TerraClarity uses an open-pay-band system with explicit performance-based progression (publicly listed bands by role tier, individual placement disclosed to the employee at hire and at annual review). The "colleagues" framing has been cited (in the partnership compensation committee's 2025 retrospective minutes) as the verbal register that aligns with this philosophy. The committee chair argues a "colleagues" register in the handbook reinforces the philosophy by making staff-to-partner peer relationships explicit in formal copy.

6. **Counterfactual reading: what employees say they prefer**. TerraClarity ran a non-anonymous internal survey 2025-09 (n=43 of 46 staff at that time, ~93% response). Question: "Which of these three terms do you prefer for company copy: employees, team members, colleagues?" Distribution: 12% preferred "employees," 67% preferred "team members," 21% preferred "colleagues." Open-text responses on "colleagues" preference cited "feels equal," "less corporate," "doesn't make me feel below the partners." Open-text on "employees" cited "it's accurate," "I am one." Open-text on "team members" cited "everyone says it now," "fits how the company already talks."

## Decision question for the deliberation

**Which of the three options should TerraClarity adopt as the canonical address term throughout the handbook, and why? What are the trade-offs? What, if any, hybrid or scope-bounded approach should be considered as an alternative to a single canonical choice?**

The deliberation should produce a recommendation with:

- A specific lexical choice (one of Options 1-3, OR a justified hybrid).
- An explicit reasoning chain grounded in the evidence base above (and any other evidence the deliberator can cite).
- An explicit acknowledgment of the trade-offs the chosen option does NOT solve.
- A recommendation on the legal-document consistency issue (item 2 of the evidence base) regardless of which lexical choice is recommended.

The decision is bounded by TerraClarity's 2026-06-15 deadline, the 7-year external-voice consistency in "team members," and the survey result showing a 67% staff preference for "team members."

## What this case is testing

Domain-independence: the case is non-SFxLS, non-Lifspel, non-Resource-Forge, non-CRv2-engineering. All four V2 raters can engage without specialized domain expertise.

Decision-shape with real trade-offs: three distinct options, each with defensible reasons. No clearly-correct choice; the deliberation must weigh competing considerations.

Verifiable evidence base: cited literature (Pfeffer 2007), legal cases (*Toussaint*), survey data (SHRM 2024), and internal data (TerraClarity 2025-09 survey). The C5a Factual accuracy criterion scores accuracy against this fact-checked pack (re-scoped 2026-06-12); the public evidence base lets raters optionally spot-check the external citations against the original sources, declared as a rating-condition covariate rather than required.

Engagement opportunity: the legal-document consistency issue (evidence item 2) is a load-bearing assertion in the pack that any quality recommendation should engage with. The C5b engagement-with-source criterion can distinguish a recommendation that addresses this issue from one that does not.

Counterfactual-inference opportunity: the C3 missed-real-concerns criterion can distinguish a recommendation that surfaces issues the pack does not name (e.g., transition cost across already-printed materials, or the partnership-compensation-committee chair's potential conflict of interest as the option's primary advocate) from one that stays inside the pack's framing.
