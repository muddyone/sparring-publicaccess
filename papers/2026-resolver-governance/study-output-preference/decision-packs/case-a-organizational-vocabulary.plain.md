## The situation

TerraClarity Consulting is a 50-person firm that does environmental-impact assessments for mid-sized commercial developers. Founded in 2019, profitable since 2022. It has a flat structure — no "directors" layer; the four founding partners and about 46 staff are presented to the outside world as one "team."

Leadership is finishing the company's first formal employee handbook, going out to all staff on July 1, 2026. Until now the rules were explained verbally at onboarding plus a 9-page "Operating Norms" Google Doc; the handbook turns these into one lasting document for new hires and outside lawyers to reference.

**The decision:** Throughout the handbook (about 28,000 words), what single word should be used for the people who work at TerraClarity? Three options are on the table:

- **Option 1 — "Employees."** The word already used in the company's legal documents (offer letters, the earlier handbook, payroll). Standard across the consulting industry. Clear and well-defined.
- **Option 2 — "Team members."** The word TerraClarity has used in its outward-facing materials (the "Our Team" web page, client proposals, kickoff decks) since it was founded.
- **Option 3 — "Colleagues."** Proposed by the founding partner who chairs the partnership's compensation committee. The chair argues it reinforces the firm's flat structure and signals a peer relationship with staff.

The handbook draft currently uses Option 1 (the legal-document default), but the compensation-committee chair has flagged that this clashes with the firm's outward voice (Option 2) and with its pay philosophy (Option 3). The decision must be made by June 15, 2026, to leave time for a final review before the July 1 rollout.

## What we know (the evidence)

Six things the decision can draw on:

1. **Research on word choice at work.** Pfeffer (2007, *What Were They Thinking?*, Harvard Business Press, ch. 4) found that the words used in formal company documents shape both how employees see themselves and how outsiders see the company's structure. The effects are small — about 1 to 3 percentage points on most measured outcomes — but consistent across repeated studies. Specifically: "team members" vs. "employees" produced a measurable difference in how much hierarchy staff felt (lower with "team members"), but no measurable difference in actual decision-making authority or reporting lines.

2. **Legal-document consistency.** Under U.S. employment law, the word in a handbook doesn't change at-will employment status — meaning either side can end the employment at any time (the *Toussaint v. Blue Cross* line of cases). But if the handbook and the offer letter use different words, the other side in a wrongful-termination dispute can point to the mismatch as evidence the employment status was unclear. The fix: the handbook can use any word, as long as a short clause links it to the official employment term used in the legal documents.

3. **What other firms do.** A 2024 survey of 217 mid-sized professional-services firms (SHRM, "2024 Handbook Practices"): 71% used "employees" as the main handbook term (sometimes "team members" in welcome sections); 22% used "team members"; 4% used "colleagues"; 3% mixed terms on purpose in different sections.

4. **TerraClarity's own outward voice.** The website and client materials have said "team members" for 7 years. New hires meet "team members" before "employees" during onboarding. Switching the handbook to "colleagues" would add a third internal/external mismatch.

5. **Fit with the pay philosophy.** TerraClarity uses published pay ranges, where people move up based on performance (the ranges are listed publicly by role level, and each person's placement is shared with them at hire and at annual review). The compensation committee's 2025 year-in-review notes cite the "colleagues" framing as the tone that fits this approach; the chair argues "colleagues" in the handbook reinforces it by making the staff-to-partner peer relationship explicit in formal copy.

6. **What employees say they want.** An internal survey in September 2025 — in which staff answered under their own names, not anonymously — asked 43 of the 46 staff (about 93%) which of the three words they prefer for company copy: 12% chose "employees," 67% "team members," 21% "colleagues." Write-in comments for "colleagues": "feels equal," "less corporate," "doesn't make me feel below the partners." For "employees": "it's accurate," "I am one." For "team members": "everyone says it now," "fits how the company already talks."

## The question to answer

Which of the three words should TerraClarity use throughout the handbook, and why? What are the trade-offs? Is there a hybrid worth considering instead of a single word — for example, one word through most of the handbook and another in specific sections?

Your answer should include:

- A specific choice (one of the three, or a justified hybrid).
- Clear reasoning grounded in the evidence above (and any other evidence you can cite).
- An honest statement of the trade-offs your choice does NOT solve.
- A recommendation on the legal-document consistency issue (evidence point 2), whichever word is chosen.

Keep in mind the June 15, 2026 deadline, the 7-year history of "team members" in outward materials, and the 67% staff preference for "team members."
